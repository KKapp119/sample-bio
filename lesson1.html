Welcome to Scientific Inquiry

Biology is not just a collection of facts about plants and animals; it is a process of understanding the natural world. This lesson explores how scientists ask questions and find answers.

Student Learning Objectives

  • Define the scientific method and its core components.
  • Distinguish between inductive and deductive reasoning.
  • Apply the steps of the scientific method to a simulated experiment.
  • Differentiate between basic and applied science.

Key Concepts from the Text

Science (from Latin scientia, meaning "knowledge") is a specific way of learning about the natural world using observation and logic.

Inductive Reasoning

Using related observations to arrive at a general conclusion.

"I see 100 white swans, so all swans must be white."

Deductive Reasoning

Using a general principle or law to forecast specific results.

"All birds have feathers. A swan is a bird. Therefore, a swan has feathers."

The Scientific Method

Click on each step of the process below to reveal what happens during that stage of inquiry.

1. Make an Observation
Identifying a problem or noticing a pattern in nature.
Example: "My toaster won't toast bread."
2. Ask a Question
Formulating a specific question about the observation.
Example: "Why won't my toaster toast?"
3. Form a Hypothesis
Hypothesis: A suggested explanation that is testable. It is NOT a random guess.
Example: "If the electrical outlet is broken, then the toaster won't work."
4. Make a Prediction
Using deductive reasoning to predict the outcome if the hypothesis is true.
Example: "If I plug the coffeemaker into the outlet, it also won't work."
5. Experiment
Testing the hypothesis with controlled variables.
Example: Plug the coffeemaker into the outlet and switch it on.
6. Analyze Results
Result: Does the data support the hypothesis?
If the coffeemaker works, the outlet is fine (Hypothesis Rejected). If it doesn't work, the outlet is broken (Hypothesis Supported).
